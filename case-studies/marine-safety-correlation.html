Executive Summary

Maritime safety data is fragmented across national investigation authorities, each with different reporting formats, classification schemes, and access methods. An operator or insurer seeking a comprehensive safety picture must manually search multiple databases with no way to detect patterns that span jurisdictions.

We built an automated multi-source ingestion and correlation platform that unifies incident records from the Canadian Transportation Safety Board (TSB), UK Marine Accident Investigation Branch (MAIB), US Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement (BSEE), OSHA, and EPA Toxics Release Inventory into a single queryable database. The platform includes fuzzy matching for cross-source deduplication, configurable risk scoring, and automated trend detection across 269,000+ records spanning 50 years.

The Challenge

Fragmented Data Silos

Each national authority maintains its own incident database with unique schemas, severity classifications, and access mechanisms. TSB Canada uses web-searchable investigation reports, MAIB UK publishes structured annual datasets, and BSEE provides bulk data via FOIA downloads. There is no unified interface.

Inconsistent Classification

Incident types are labelled differently across sources: what TSB calls a "COLLISION" may appear as "Contact" in MAIB data and "Equipment Strike" in BSEE records. Severity scales, vessel type categories, and geographic references vary between every authority.

Scale of Data

With 53,000+ maritime incidents and 98,000+ offshore HSE records, manual cross-referencing is not feasible. Identifying whether the same vessel or event appears in multiple databases requires automated matching across name variations, approximate locations, and overlapping date windows.

Our Approach

1

Automated Multi-Source Ingestion

Built dedicated importers for each authority (TSB, MAIB, BSEE, OSHA, EPA TRI) that handle source-specific formats—HTML scraping for web-published reports, CSV parsing for bulk datasets, and API integration where available. Each importer normalizes records into a common schema with standardized incident types, severity levels, and geographic coordinates.

2

Fuzzy Cross-Source Correlation Engine

Developed a multi-strategy matching engine that identifies related incidents across databases using: IMO number matching (exact), vessel name similarity (token-set fuzzy ratio with 80% threshold), geographic proximity (haversine distance within 50 km), and temporal alignment (3-day window). A weighted confidence score combines all signals to rank potential matches.

3

Pattern Detection & Trend Analysis

Applied statistical analysis across the unified dataset to detect temporal trends, geographic hotspots, and recurring incident patterns. Root cause analysis extracts contributing factors from narrative text fields using NLP classification, enabling cross-jurisdictional pattern identification.

4

Three-Dimensional Risk Scoring

Implemented a configurable risk index that scores activities across three dimensions—acute safety (fatalities, injuries, incident frequency), chronic environmental (toxic releases, spill volumes), and regulatory compliance (violation rates). Scores are normalized to a 1–10 scale with percentile ranking, sliceable by field, water depth band, and time period.

Correlation Engine Architecture

The matching engine uses a weighted multi-signal approach:

  • IMO Number (40% weight): Exact match on 7-digit IMO identifier provides highest-confidence vessel linkage
  • Vessel Name (25% weight): Token-set fuzzy ratio handles abbreviations, spelling variations, and name changes
  • Location (20% weight): Haversine distance calculation with configurable radius (default 50 km)
  • Date (15% weight): Temporal proximity within configurable window (default 3 days)

Results

Unified Safety Intelligence Platform

Consolidated 269,302 records from 5 databases into a single queryable platform. An analyst can now search across all sources simultaneously, filtering by incident type, date range, severity, geographic region, or vessel. What previously required manual searches across 5 separate systems now takes seconds.

Cross-Jurisdictional Pattern Detection

Trend analysis revealed that collision incidents account for 55% of all maritime events across both Canadian and UK waters—a pattern invisible when viewing each authority's data in isolation. The platform also identified a reporting volume increase starting in 2021 (from ~1,200 to ~2,500 annual incidents in TSB data), flagging it as a reporting methodology change rather than a genuine safety deterioration.

Configurable Risk Scoring

The three-dimensional risk index (acute, chronic, compliance) enables operators to assess risk profiles across activities, fields, and water depth bands. Weights are configurable via YAML, allowing clients to adjust the scoring to their specific risk appetite. The 1–10 scale with descriptive bands (Low, Moderate, Elevated, High, Critical) provides an intuitive framework for decision-making.

Data Limitations & Caveats

  • Source coverage: Currently 5 of 10+ planned authorities are ingested. USCG MISLE, NTSB, IMO GISIS, ATSB, and EMSA importers are built but awaiting data refresh.
  • Severity uniformity: TSB and MAIB data currently use a single severity level; granular severity differentiation is an ongoing enrichment effort.
  • Vessel linkage: 13% of maritime incidents have vessel identifiers linked; coverage improves as additional IMO number matching is applied.
  • Temporal bias: Older records (pre-2000) have fewer structured fields. Trend analysis should account for evolving reporting standards.
  • Geographic scope: Maritime data covers Canadian and UK waters. US coverage is primarily offshore oil & gas operations (BSEE jurisdiction).

Key Takeaways

For Maritime Operators & Insurers

  • Fragmented safety databases create blind spots; unified platforms reveal patterns that no single source can show
  • Automated ingestion and normalization eliminates the manual effort of cross-referencing authorities
  • Configurable risk scoring enables data-driven safety investment prioritization

For Safety Professionals

  • Fuzzy matching across vessel names, locations, and dates identifies related incidents that exact-match searches miss
  • Transparent methodology with documented weights and caveats builds credibility with regulators
  • 50 years of historical data enables long-term trend detection beyond annual reporting cycles
